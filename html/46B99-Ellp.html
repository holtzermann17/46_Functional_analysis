ell^p


Let 𝔽 be either ℝ or ℂ, and let p∈ℝ with p≥1. We define ℓp to be the set of all sequences (ai)i≥0 in 𝔽 such that

∑i=0∞|ai|p

converges.

We also define ℓ∞ to be the set of all bounded (http://planetmath.org/BoundedInterval) sequences (ai)i≥0 with norm given by

∥(ai)∥∞=sup⁡{|ai|:i≥0}.

By defining addition and scalar multiplication pointwise, ℓp⁢(𝔽) and ℓ∞⁢(𝔽) have a natural vector space stucture. That the sum of two elements on ℓp⁢(𝔽) is again an element in ℓp⁢(𝔽) follows from Minkowski inequality (see below). We can make ℓp into a normed vector space, by defining the norm as

∥(ai)∥p=(∑i=0∞|ai|p)1/p.

The normed vector spaces ℓ∞ and ℓp for p≥1 are complete under these norms, making them into Banach spaces. Moreover, ℓ2 is a Hilbert space under the inner product

⟨(ai),(bi)⟩=∑i=0∞ai⁢bi¯

where x¯ denotes the complex conjugate of x.

For p>1 the (continuous) dual space of ℓp is ℓq where 1p+1q=1, and the dual space of ℓ1 is ℓ∞.

Properties

  1. 1.

    If a=(a0,a1,…)∈ℓp⁢(𝔽) for 1≤p<∞, then limk→∞⁡ak=0. (proof. (http://planetmath.org/ThenA_kto0IfSum_k1inftyA_kConverges))

  2. 2.

    For 1≤p<∞, ℓp⁢(𝔽) is separable, and ℓ∞⁢(𝔽) is not separable.

  3. 3.

    Minkowski inequality. If a,b∈ℓp⁢(𝔽) where p≥1, then

    ∥a+b∥p≤∥a∥p+∥b∥p.
