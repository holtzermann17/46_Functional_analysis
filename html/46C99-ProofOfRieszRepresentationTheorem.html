proof of Riesz representation theorem


Existence - If f=0 we can just take u=0 and thereby have f⁢(x)=0=⟨x,0⟩ for all x∈ℋ.

Suppose now f≠0, i.e. K⁢e⁢r⁢f≠ℋ.

Recall that, since f is continuous (http://planetmath.org/ContinuousMap), K⁢e⁢r⁢f is a closed subspace of ℋ (continuity of f implies that f-1⁢(0) is closed in ℋ). It then follows from the orthogonal decomposition theorem that

ℋ=K⁢e⁢r⁢f⊕(K⁢e⁢r⁢f)⟂

and as K⁢e⁢r⁢f≠ℋ we can find z∈(K⁢e⁢r⁢f)⟂ such that ∥z∥=1.

It follows easily from the linearity of f that for every x∈ℋ we have

f⁢(x)⁢z-f⁢(z)⁢x∈K⁢e⁢r⁢f

and since z∈(K⁢e⁢r⁢f)⟂

      0 = ⟨f⁢(x)⁢z-f⁢(z)⁢x,z⟩
= f⁢(x)⁢⟨z,z⟩-f⁢(z)⁢⟨x,z⟩
= f⁢(x)⁢∥z∥2-⟨x,f⁢(z)¯⁢z⟩
= f⁢(x)-⟨x,f⁢(z)¯⁢z⟩

which implies

f⁢(x)=⟨x,f⁢(z)¯⁢z⟩.

The theorem then follows by taking u=f⁢(z)¯⁢z.

Uniqueness - Suppose there were u1,u2∈ℋ such that for every x∈ℋ

f⁢(x)=⟨x,u1⟩=⟨x,u2⟩.

Then ⟨x,u1-u2⟩=0 for every x∈ℋ. Taking x=u1-u2 we obtain ∥u1-u2∥2=0, which implies u1=u2. □
